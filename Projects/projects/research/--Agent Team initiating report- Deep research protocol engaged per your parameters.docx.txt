### Gig Economy Data (National Geographic – Southeast Asia focus)
More than half the workforce in most Southeast Asian countries is informal. Cambodia: 94%. Myanmar: ~80%. Thailand: 64%. Gig platforms (superapps like Grab) let informal workers join as drivers or delivery partners using only a smartphone—no traditional barriers. Pandemic lockdowns made steady informal jobs harder; gig delivery became essential for goods to homebound people.


**Pure experiential elements**:
- Safety toolkit in-app (report incidents, emergency call, notify loved ones) + personal accident insurance during trips.
- Training programs (digital literacy, technical upskilling, financial literacy) via app or in-person, partnered with governments—improves earnings, money management, job prospects.
- Healthcare costs have pushed up to 65 million in the region into poverty; gig access counters this for informal workers.

### Remote Work / Work-from-Home Turning into Culture (National Geographic articles)
Pre-pandemic baseline: 7.3 million Americans self-identified as digital nomads; over one-third of jobs could be fully remote. Post-pandemic: 82% of company leaders plan to keep remote options for at least part of the workforce. Half of U.S. adults with jobs worked from home during peak pandemic (more than double pre-pandemic levels). 20% of CFOs planned permanent remote for ≥20% of staff. 54% expect to continue some remote post-pandemic.


**Real lived experiences (direct quotes & stories)**:
- Arthur Deane (Google senior manager): Worked remotely from D.C. apartment for months, then tested Aruba trip. “I don’t see returning to an office full-time being the way of the future. The pandemic has taught us that we can be productive without being in the office, Monday to Friday, nine to five.” Plans longer returns.
- Kiwan Michael Anderson (PR manager, New York): Extended Bermuda family visit from 2 weeks to 9 weeks. Better Wi-Fi than Brooklyn apartment + 1-hour time-zone edge for productivity. - Natalie Serianni (Seattle English professor): Second-grader sneaked into Zoom meeting unnoticed (headphones + tall chair); colleagues laughed at the surprise kid appearance.
